The question of who should have ultimate authority over school curricula — professional educators or elected governments — touches on fundamental tensions between pedagogical expertise, democratic accountability, and the contested purposes of public education. I largely agree that teachers and educational professionals should bear the primary responsibility for curriculum design, though I also recognise the legitimate role of government in establishing broad educational frameworks.

The case for teacher-led curriculum development is compelling. Classroom educators possess an intimate, day-to-day understanding of their students’ learning needs, developmental stages, cultural contexts, and academic challenges that no politician, however well-intentioned, can replicate from a distance. Curricula designed by practising educators are more likely to be pedagogically coherent, age-appropriate, and responsive to the realities of the modern knowledge economy. Furthermore, when teachers are entrusted with meaningful professional autonomy, research consistently shows improvements in both teacher motivation and student engagement, as learning becomes more dynamic and contextually relevant.

However, it would be unrealistic to advocate for complete governmental disengagement from education. Elected governments have a legitimate responsibility to ensure that public education serves the broader national interest, promotes civic values, and provides equitable access to essential knowledge across all schools and regions. Without a degree of centralised oversight, there is a risk of significant variations in educational quality and content, potentially exacerbating existing social inequalities.

The optimal model, in my view, is a collaborative one: national governments set broad strategic frameworks and overarching educational goals, while individual schools and teachers retain the professional freedom to determine how those goals are best achieved within their specific contexts.

In conclusion, I strongly agree that teachers should play the primary role in curriculum design. Government involvement, while necessary, should function as a guiding framework rather than a prescriptive straitjacket.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~257 Từ)**

School subjects are currently decided by governments in many countries. However, some people believe that teachers, not politicians, should have this responsibility. I strongly agree with this view.

First, teachers understand students much better than politicians do. Teachers work with students every day and know their strengths, weaknesses, and learning needs. Because of this direct experience, teachers are in a much better position to decide what subjects and topics will be most useful and appropriate for students. Politicians, on the other hand, are rarely inside classrooms and may not fully understand the realities of education.

Second, letting teachers design the curriculum can make education more flexible and effective. When teachers have more freedom, they can adapt the content to suit the specific needs of their students and the local community. This can make lessons more interesting and relevant, which can help students learn better and enjoy school more.

Third, giving teachers more responsibility can also improve their motivation and job satisfaction. When teachers feel trusted and valued as professionals, they are likely to put more effort into their work, which ultimately benefits the students.

However, I think the government should still have some role in education. For example, they should make sure that all schools meet certain basic standards and that students learn key subjects like maths, science, and languages. But the detailed content should be decided by teachers.

In conclusion, teachers should be the main people responsible for deciding what students learn. This would lead to a more effective, flexible, and student-centred education system.
