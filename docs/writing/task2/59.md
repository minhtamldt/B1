🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~300 Từ)**

The argument that more young people should be encouraged to pursue vocational training, rather than defaulting to a university degree, is one that I find broadly persuasive, though not to the exclusion of academic education.

The most compelling case for expanding vocational training lies in the persistent and widening skills gap in many modern economies. In countries across the world, there is an acute and growing shortage of qualified skilled tradespeople — including electricians, plumbers, carpenters, and healthcare technicians — while simultaneously, a significant proportion of university graduates find themselves either unemployed or working in positions entirely unrelated to their degree. This mismatch between educational supply and labour market demand represents an enormous inefficiency that vocational training is uniquely positioned to address. Skilled tradespeople are not only in high demand but frequently earn salaries that rival, or even surpass, those of degree-holding white-collar professionals, often with considerably less student debt.

Furthermore, vocational training pathways are more time-efficient and cost-effective. A plumbing apprenticeship, for instance, can typically be completed within two to three years at a fraction of the cost of a four-year university degree. This allows young people to enter the workforce sooner, contribute economically, and build financial independence at an earlier stage of life.

It would, however, be reductive to dismiss university education entirely. Professions such as medicine, law, engineering, and scientific research require the depth of theoretical knowledge that only sustained academic study can provide. The key insight is that both pathways are valuable and essential — what is needed is a cultural shift in how society perceives and values skilled trades, elevating them to the same status as university-educated professions.

In conclusion, a greater emphasis on vocational training would benefit individuals, economies, and societies, but must be pursued alongside, not at the expense of, quality academic education.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~258 Từ)**

Many young people go to university to get a degree, but some argue that vocational training should be encouraged more. I agree that more people should be encouraged to do vocational training, because it provides many practical benefits for both individuals and society.

First, there is a serious shortage of skilled workers in many countries. Jobs like electricians, plumbers, and car mechanics are in high demand, but there are not enough qualified workers to fill these positions. If more young people received vocational training, this shortage could be reduced, and society would function more smoothly.

Second, vocational training is often faster and cheaper than university education. A vocational course usually takes two or three years and costs much less than a four-year university degree. This means young people can start working and earning money sooner, without being burdened with large debts.

Third, skilled workers can earn very good salaries. In many countries, experienced electricians and plumbers earn just as much as, or even more than, many university graduates. This shows that vocational careers can be just as rewarding as academic careers.

Of course, university education is still important for some professions, such as medicine, law, and scientific research. However, not everyone needs a university degree to have a successful and fulfilling career.

In conclusion, encouraging more young people to pursue vocational training would benefit individuals and the economy. Society needs to recognise that skilled trade careers are just as valuable and important as university-educated professions.
