🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~305 Từ)**

The debate over whether university students should enjoy unrestricted freedom of subject choice or be directed toward economically valuable disciplines reflects a deeper tension between individual self-determination and societal utility. While I appreciate the genuine merits on both sides of this debate, I believe that personal academic freedom is a fundamental right that should be protected, even amid pragmatic economic considerations.

Advocates for academic freedom argue, persuasively, that forcing students into disciplines they have no passion for produces disengaged, unmotivated learners who are unlikely to reach their full professional potential. The history of human innovation is replete with examples of breakthroughs that emerged not from narrowly directed technical training, but from the interdisciplinary curiosity of individuals who were allowed to follow their intellectual interests freely. Furthermore, ostensibly “non-practical” subjects such as philosophy, literature, and history cultivate critical thinking, analytical writing, and ethical reasoning — competencies that are highly transferable across virtually every professional domain.

Conversely, proponents of restricting students to high-value fields contend that universities bear a responsibility to meet present and future labour market demands, and that public funding for education should prioritise disciplines with clear societal returns, such as engineering, medicine, and computer science. They argue that graduates in saturated or low-demand fields frequently face unemployment or chronic underemployment, imposing costs on both the individual and the public purse.

In my view, the most constructive solution is neither full restriction nor entirely unfettered choice. Universities should offer robust academic freedom, while also providing informed guidance on labour market realities during the enrolment process, empowering students to make well-informed decisions.

In conclusion, academic freedom should be preserved, but matched with transparency and informed career guidance, ensuring that students’ passions and economic realities are both given due consideration.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~258 Từ)**

There are two main opinions about what university students should study. Some believe that students should be free to choose any subject they like, while others think they should only study subjects that are useful for the economy, like science and technology. In my opinion, students should have the freedom to choose, but they also need information to make good decisions.

On the one hand, giving students the freedom to choose their subjects has many advantages. When students study something they are passionate about, they are more motivated and work harder. They are also more creative and innovative because they enjoy what they are learning. In addition, subjects like history, philosophy, and literature develop important skills such as critical thinking, communication, and problem-solving, which are valuable in many jobs.

On the other hand, some people argue that students should focus on subjects that are in demand in the job market, such as engineering, medicine, and computer science. They say that graduates from other fields often struggle to find good jobs. Therefore, restricting students to useful subjects could reduce unemployment and benefit the economy.

In my view, the best approach is to let students choose freely, but to also give them honest information about job opportunities in different fields. This way, students can choose subjects they love while also understanding the real-world consequences.

In conclusion, academic freedom should be respected, but universities should also support students with career advice and information. This balances individual fulfilment with the needs of the economy.
