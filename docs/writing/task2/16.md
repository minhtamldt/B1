🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~300 Từ)**

The rise of vegetarianism as a lifestyle choice reflects a growing global consciousness about personal health and environmental sustainability. While I fully appreciate the sincerity and the merits of this viewpoint, I believe that framing vegetarianism as universally beneficial requires careful nuance.

From a health perspective, there is compelling scientific evidence that a well-planned plant-based diet can reduce the risk of chronic diseases, including heart disease, type 2 diabetes, and certain forms of cancer. By eliminating saturated fats found predominantly in red meat and processed meats, vegetarians often maintain healthier cholesterol levels and body weight. Furthermore, diets rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and legumes provide an abundance of fibre, antioxidants, and phytonutrients that support long-term vitality.

The environmental case for vegetarianism is equally persuasive. The livestock industry is responsible for approximately 14.5% of global greenhouse gas emissions, according to the Food and Agriculture Organisation of the United Nations. The land, water, and energy resources required to raise animals for food far outstrip those needed to produce equivalent plant-based nutrition. A global shift toward plant-based diets, even a partial one, could substantially reduce our collective ecological footprint.

However, it is essential to acknowledge that vegetarianism is not universally accessible. In many communities — particularly indigenous populations and those in regions with limited agricultural diversity — animal products are a dietary and cultural necessity. Moreover, poorly planned vegetarian diets can lead to deficiencies in vital nutrients, including B12, iron, and omega-3 fatty acids.

In conclusion, while vegetarianism offers genuine and significant benefits for health and the environment, it is most effective when adopted as a personal, well-informed choice rather than imposed as a blanket solution.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~258 Từ)**

Recently, more and more people are choosing to become vegetarians. They believe that not eating meat or fish is good for their health and for the environment. In my opinion, I partly agree with this viewpoint, but I also think there are some limitations.

First of all, I think vegetarianism has many health benefits. People who eat a lot of vegetables, fruits, and whole grains tend to be healthier. They have lower risks of serious diseases like heart disease and diabetes. This is because plant-based foods have less fat and more fibre and vitamins.

Second, vegetarianism is good for the environment. The meat industry uses a lot of land, water, and energy, and it produces a lot of greenhouse gases. If more people become vegetarians, we can reduce damage to our planet.

However, I also think that a vegetarian diet is not suitable for everyone. Some people live in places where vegetables and fruits are hard to find or too expensive. For these people, eating meat and fish is necessary. Also, if people do not plan their vegetarian diet carefully, they may not get all the nutrition they need, for example protein, iron, and vitamin B12.

In conclusion, I think vegetarianism is a good choice for many people because it benefits both health and the environment. However, it should be a personal choice, not something forced on everyone. People should be educated about how to follow a healthy and balanced vegetarian diet.
