🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~300 Từ)**

Museums serve as irreplaceable custodians of cultural heritage, offering society a tangible connection to its past. The question of whether charging an admission fee undermines this mission or, conversely, safeguards it, is one that merits careful consideration. In my view, the disadvantages of charging entry fees to museums broadly outweigh the advantages.

Proponents of admission charges argue, convincingly, that the revenue generated is essential to cover the substantial operating costs of museums, including the preservation of delicate artefacts, the salaries of expert curators, and the development of new, engaging exhibitions. Without a sustainable funding model, many institutions would face the grim prospect of closure or deterioration. Furthermore, some economists contend that placing a monetary value on museum access incentivises visitors to engage more thoughtfully with exhibits, rather than treating them as mere tourist attractions.

However, the case against admission fees is considerably more compelling. Museums are, at their core, public institutions serving the collective educational and cultural interests of society. When financial barriers are erected, access becomes disproportionately restricted for children, students, low-income families, and marginalised communities — precisely those who stand to benefit most profoundly from cultural education. Evidence from countries such as the United Kingdom, where national museums have offered free admission since 2001, demonstrates that free access dramatically increases visitor diversity and overall attendance without compromising institutional quality.

Moreover, governments have both the capacity and the responsibility to fund cultural institutions through taxation and public grants, rather than imposing financial burdens on individual visitors.

In conclusion, while admission fees provide a degree of financial stability, the social and educational costs of exclusion are too high. Museums should remain freely accessible to all, with alternative funding models ensuring their long-term sustainability.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~255 Từ)**

Museums are very important places where people can learn about history and culture. However, there is a debate about whether museums should charge visitors an admission fee. In my opinion, the disadvantages of charging a fee are greater than the advantages.

On the one hand, charging an admission fee has some benefits. The money collected can help museums pay for the costs of maintaining exhibits, buying new artefacts, and paying staff. Without this income, some museums might not have enough money to continue operating.

On the other hand, I think the disadvantages are much more significant. First of all, admission fees make it harder for many people to visit museums, especially children, students, and people with low incomes. Education and culture should be available to everyone, not just those who can afford to pay. Second, when museums are free, more people visit them. This means more people can learn about history and culture, which is good for society as a whole. For example, in the United Kingdom, major national museums stopped charging fees in 2001, and visitor numbers increased significantly.

I believe that governments should take responsibility for funding museums through public money. This way, museums can remain free and open to everyone.

In conclusion, while admission fees can help museums financially, the social and educational benefits of free access are much more important. Museums should be free so that everyone in society can learn from them.
