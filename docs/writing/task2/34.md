🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~300 Từ)**

The proposition that ex-prisoners represent the most effective educators of teenagers about the consequences of crime is one that I find largely, though not unconditionally, convincing. Their unique lived experience endows them with a form of credibility and authenticity that no textbook, classroom lecture, or law enforcement professional can easily replicate.

The most compelling argument in favour of this view is the unrivalled persuasive power of genuine personal testimony. When a former prisoner speaks candidly to young people about the devastating consequences of a criminal lifestyle — the loss of freedom, the fracturing of family relationships, the permanent stigma on one’s record, and the psychological burden of incarceration — the message carries an emotional weight that is simply inaccessible to those who have never experienced it. Research into crime prevention programmes consistently demonstrates that peer-based and experience-based interventions produce significantly stronger behavioural outcomes among at-risk youth than abstract, authority-delivered warnings. Teenagers, who are naturally sceptical of figures of authority, are far more likely to listen to, identify with, and be genuinely moved by someone who has “been there”.

However, it would be naive to suggest that all ex-prisoners are equally suitable or effective in this role. Individuals who have not undergone genuine rehabilitation, who lack communication skills, or who harbour unresolved resentment may inadvertently glamourise criminal behaviour or fail to convey a constructive message. It is therefore essential that any programme utilising ex-prisoners as educators operates within a structured framework, with professional support and rigorous selection criteria.

In conclusion, ex-prisoners can indeed be among the most powerful and effective voices in crime prevention education, provided they are properly supported, screened, and integrated into thoughtfully designed programmes.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~258 Từ)**

Some people believe that ex-prisoners are the most effective people to teach teenagers about the dangers of crime. In my opinion, I largely agree with this idea, though there are some important limitations to consider.

First of all, ex-prisoners have real personal experience of crime and its consequences. When they share their stories with teenagers, their words are more powerful and believable than a lesson from a teacher or police officer. For example, an ex-prisoner can talk honestly about how they lost their freedom, damaged their family relationships, and ruined their future. This kind of first-hand account is something teenagers can relate to and take seriously.

Second, teenagers often do not listen to figures of authority. However, they may be more open to listening to someone who has actually been through the criminal justice system. The personal and emotional nature of these stories can be very effective in discouraging young people from making bad choices.

However, not all ex-prisoners are suitable for this role. Some may not have fully reformed or may accidentally make crime sound exciting or attractive. Therefore, it is important to choose ex-prisoners who have clearly changed their lives for the better and who have good communication skills.

In conclusion, ex-prisoners can be among the most effective people to educate teenagers about crime, because of the power of their personal experience. However, they should be carefully selected and work within a structured educational programme to ensure their message is positive and helpful.
