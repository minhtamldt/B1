🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~305 Từ)**

The ethics of keeping wild animals in captivity is one of the most emotionally charged and philosophically complex debates in contemporary conservation discourse. While the practice of maintaining zoological parks has attracted fierce condemnation from animal rights advocates, proponents argue that modern zoos serve a vital conservation and educational function that would be difficult to replicate through any other means.

The case against zoos rests on the foundational principle that wild animals have an inherent right to freedom and to live within their natural habitats. Confining sentient creatures within artificial enclosures, however well-designed, inflicts profound psychological suffering. Behaviours such as repetitive pacing, stereotypy, and self-harm — collectively known as zoochosis — are widely documented in captive animals and constitute incontrovertible evidence of severe psychological distress. Critics further contend that many animals in zoos are not endangered species requiring conservation, but rather species kept purely for commercial entertainment.

On the other hand, the contribution of accredited zoos to global conservation efforts is substantial and well-documented. Many zoos fund and participate in breeding programmes for critically endangered species such as the giant panda, the Arabian oryx, and the California condor, several of which have been successfully reintroduced into the wild directly as a result of zoo-based conservation work. Moreover, zoos serve as powerful educational platforms, fostering public awareness of biodiversity loss and environmental stewardship, particularly among younger generations.

On balance, I believe that the future of zoos should lie in evolving away from entertainment-oriented models toward genuine conservation sanctuaries — spacious, naturalistic environments that prioritise animal welfare above all else.

In conclusion, while the practice of keeping animals in captivity raises legitimate ethical concerns, responsibly managed zoos that prioritise conservation and animal welfare can make a net positive contribution to the natural world.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~260 Từ)**

Keeping animals in zoos is a topic that many people disagree about. Some think it is always wrong, while others believe it has many benefits. In my opinion, keeping animals in zoos is acceptable only if it is done to help animals and educate the public, not just for entertainment.

On the one hand, some people strongly believe that keeping animals in zoos is wrong. They say that wild animals should be free and should live in their natural habitats. When animals are kept in small cages or enclosures, they can feel very stressed and unhappy. They may also develop unusual behaviours, such as walking in circles or injuring themselves. This shows that captivity causes them suffering.

On the other hand, others argue that zoos play an important role in conservation. Many modern zoos help to protect endangered species by running breeding programmes. Some animals, like the giant panda and the Arabian oryx, have been saved from extinction because of zoo conservation efforts. Zoos also educate the public, especially children, about wildlife and the importance of protecting the environment. This can inspire people to care more about nature.

In conclusion, I think zoos can be beneficial if they focus on animal welfare and conservation rather than entertainment. Animals should be kept in large, natural environments where they can live comfortably. Zoos that do not meet high welfare standards should be closed. With proper management, zoos can be a valuable tool for protecting wildlife and educating society.
