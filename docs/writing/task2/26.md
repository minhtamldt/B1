In the hyper-competitive landscape of modern economies, the erosion of work-life balance has emerged as a pervasive and deeply consequential social problem. Far from being a matter of personal discipline, it is a systemic challenge rooted in structural economic pressures and cultural norms that demands targeted solutions from both employers and individuals.

The most fundamental driver of poor work-life balance is the relentless pressure of performance-driven work cultures. In an era defined by 24/7 digital connectivity, the boundaries between professional and personal time have become dangerously blurred. Employees in competitive industries often feel implicitly coerced into being perpetually available via smartphones and email, making genuine psychological detachment from work virtually impossible. This is further compounded by economic insecurity: in many households, particularly those with unstable incomes or mounting debt, working long hours feels not like a choice but an existential necessity.

Additionally, inadequate institutional support for working parents — such as the scarcity of affordable childcare and the lack of flexible working arrangements — forces many individuals, disproportionately women, into impossible trade-offs between career progression and family responsibility.

To address this multifaceted challenge, employers must take proactive steps: implementing and enforcing meaningful right-to-disconnect policies, normalising flexible and remote working arrangements, and cultivating organisational cultures that genuinely value employee well-being over performative busyness. At the individual level, setting non-negotiable boundaries around personal time, practising mindful time management, and actively delegating tasks can reclaim a sense of autonomy and balance.

In conclusion, the work-life imbalance crisis is driven by cultural pressures, digital intrusion, and economic insecurity. Its resolution requires structural reform at the workplace level, complemented by intentional personal strategies.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~258 Từ)**

In today’s busy world, many people find it very difficult to maintain a healthy work-life balance. This is a growing problem in modern society. There are several causes of this issue, and both individuals and employers can take steps to solve it.

First, one major cause is the pressure to work long hours. Many companies expect their employees to work beyond normal office hours, and employees feel they must do so to keep their jobs or to get a promotion. This leaves very little time for family or personal activities.

Second, technology has made things worse. Smartphones and the internet mean that people are always reachable, even after leaving the office. Many workers feel they have to reply to emails and messages outside of working hours. This makes it very hard to fully relax and enjoy personal time.

Third, high living costs are also a cause. Many families need two incomes to survive, which means both parents have to work long hours, leaving little time for children and household activities.

To solve this problem, employers can introduce more flexible working hours and allow employees to work from home some days. Companies should also have clear rules about not contacting employees outside of office hours. As individuals, people should learn to say no to extra work when it is too much. They should also plan their personal time just as carefully as their work schedule.

In conclusion, poor work-life balance is caused by overwork, technology, and financial pressure. Both employers and individuals have roles to play in creating a healthier and more balanced lifestyle.
