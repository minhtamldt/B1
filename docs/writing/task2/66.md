🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~305 Từ)**

The question of whether individuals over the age of 65 should continue to pursue new learning opportunities is a subject of significant debate. While some argue that cognitive decline makes late-life learning impractical, I firmly believe that intellectual engagement is not only possible but profoundly beneficial for the elderly.

On one hand, the perspective that it is "too late to learn" after 65 often stems from traditional views of the ageing process. Proponents of this view suggest that the brain's plasticity decreases significantly with age, making it difficult to acquire complex new skills, such as a foreign language or advanced technology. They may also point out that many seniors prefer a period of rest and leisure after a lifetime of work, rather than the mental exertion required for formal study. From this viewpoint, resources might be better spent on educating younger generations whose potential for long-term societal contribution is greater.

On the other hand, a growing body of scientific evidence supports the idea that the brain remains capable of "neuroplasticity" well into old age. Learning something new—be it a musical instrument, a craft, or digital literacy—serves as a form of "mental gymnastics" that can delay the onset of cognitive diseases like Alzheimer’s and dementia. Furthermore, lifelong learning offers immense psychological benefits. It provides seniors with a sense of purpose, combats social isolation, and helps them remain connected to a rapidly changing world. By mastering new skills, older adults can maintain their independence and continue to contribute their wisdom and experience to society in new ways.

In my opinion, the belief that learning stops at 65 is an outdated myth. While the pace of learning might change, the capacity for growth does not. Encouraging seniors to remain intellectually active is essential for their well-being and for building a more inclusive, age-agnostic society.

In conclusion, while some focus on the limitations of ageing, I believe that the benefits of lifelong learning far outweigh the challenges. We should celebrate the idea that it is never too late to expand one's horizons.

Some people think that people over 65 years old are too old to learn new things. Other people believe that it is never too late to learn and that learning is good for older people. In my opinion, I agree with the second view because learning has many benefits for everyone, regardless of their age.

On the one hand, some people say that older people have a poor memory. They think it is very hard for them to learn difficult things like new languages or how to use a computer. These people believe that after retirement, seniors should just relax and enjoy their free time with their families instead of studying.

On the other hand, many people believe that learning is a great way to keep the brain healthy. When older people learn a new hobby or a skill, their minds stay active and sharp. This can help prevent memory loss and make them feel more energetic. Also, learning new things helps seniors stay connected with the modern world. For example, if they learn how to use the internet, they can talk to their grandchildren more easily and stay in touch with friends. It also makes them feel proud of themselves.

In my opinion, I think learning is very important for elderly people. Age is just a number, and our brains can always grow if we practice. Many seniors today are going back to school or learning new crafts, and they are very happy.

In conclusion, I disagree that 65 is too late to learn. I believe that learning something new is the best way for older people to stay healthy, happy, and confident.
