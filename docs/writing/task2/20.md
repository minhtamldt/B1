🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~305 Từ)**

The question of how best to nurture the development of young children has long been a subject of lively debate among educators, psychologists, and parents alike. The assertion that engaging children in enjoyable hobbies and sports is more beneficial than encouraging them to read is one I find largely, though not unconditionally, compelling.

The case for prioritising play, hobbies, and physical activity in early childhood is grounded in well-established cognitive science. Research consistently demonstrates that young children learn most effectively through direct sensory experience and social interaction rather than passive absorption of information from text. A child who plays a team sport, for instance, simultaneously develops physical coordination, strategic thinking, emotional regulation, and the ability to collaborate and communicate — competencies that no book alone could cultivate with the same efficacy. Similarly, creative hobbies such as painting, music, or crafting stimulate neural pathways associated with problem-solving and innovation, fostering the kind of lateral thinking that is invaluable in the modern economy.

Furthermore, physical activity has been conclusively linked to improved mental health outcomes. Children who engage regularly in sport demonstrate lower rates of anxiety and depression and exhibit significantly better concentration and academic performance — suggesting that play and learning are, in fact, deeply complementary rather than mutually exclusive.

However, it would be reductive to dismiss reading entirely. Sustained engagement with books builds vocabulary, stimulates imagination, and cultivates empathy through exposure to diverse perspectives and narratives. The most holistic approach, I would argue, is one that integrates both: structured time for exploratory play and sport, alongside encouraged, never forced, reading.

In conclusion, while hobbies and sports offer advantages that books alone cannot replicate, the ideal environment for child development embraces the full spectrum of stimulating experiences.

Nowadays, many people discuss how to help children develop their skills and abilities. Some people believe that hobbies and sports are more beneficial for children than reading books. I agree with this viewpoint, but I also think that reading is still important.

First of all, doing hobbies and playing sports help children develop many important skills. For example, when children play team sports like football or basketball, they learn how to work together, communicate, and follow rules. These are very important life skills that they will need in the future. In addition, creative hobbies such as drawing, music, and crafts help children improve their imagination and problem-solving skills.

Second, physical activities are good for children's health and happiness. Children who do sports regularly are healthier and often feel happier. Research shows that active children also focus better at school and get better grades than children who do not exercise.

However, I do not think reading is unimportant. Reading helps children build a larger vocabulary and learn about the world. It also helps them use their imagination and develop empathy by learning about different people and different lives. Therefore, I think the best approach is to balance both activities: let children enjoy hobbies and sports, and also encourage them to read regularly.

In conclusion, I believe that hobbies and sports are generally more beneficial for children's development than reading alone. However, children benefit the most when they have a mix of active play, creative hobbies, and reading in their daily lives.
