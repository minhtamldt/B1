🏆 **2. BÀI MẪU C1 (~305 Từ)**

The accelerating depletion of the world’s freshwater reserves represents one of the most serious environmental and humanitarian crises of the 21st century. While fresh water has always been unevenly distributed across the globe, a convergence of powerful forces has transformed what was once a regional scarcity into an urgent worldwide emergency.

The surge in global demand for fresh water is driven by three interconnected factors. First, rapid population growth continues to place unprecedented pressure on existing water supplies, as more people require water for drinking, sanitation, and food production. Second, intensive agricultural practices are by far the most voracious consumer of freshwater resources, accounting for approximately 70% of global water withdrawals. The expansion of irrigated farming to feed a growing population creates a self-reinforcing cycle that further strains already depleted aquifers and river systems. Third, industrial development and rising living standards in emerging economies have significantly elevated per capita water consumption, as more households gain access to water-intensive appliances and lifestyle choices.

Addressing this crisis demands a coordinated response from both governments and individuals. At the policy level, governments must invest urgently in modernising water infrastructure, repairing leaking distribution networks, and subsidising the adoption of precision irrigation technologies that drastically reduce agricultural water waste. Implementing robust water pricing mechanisms that reflect the true scarcity value of the resource would also disincentivise wasteful consumption. At the individual level, seemingly small behaviour changes — shorter showers, repairing dripping taps, choosing water-efficient appliances — aggregate into meaningful conservation outcomes when adopted at scale.

In conclusion, the freshwater crisis is a product of population growth, agricultural demand, and industrial expansion. It demands urgent, multi-level action from governments and citizens alike if future water security is to be assured.

🛟 **3. BÀI MẪU B1 (~260 Từ)**

Fresh water is very important for all living things. However, in recent years, fresh water has become a serious problem worldwide. There are several main causes of this problem, and both governments and individuals must take action to solve it.

First, the world’s population is growing very fast. More people mean more demand for drinking water, cooking, and washing. This puts a lot of pressure on available water sources. Second, agriculture uses an enormous amount of fresh water. Farmers need water to grow food for billions of people, and in many countries, old and inefficient irrigation methods waste a lot of water. Third, industrial development also increases the demand for fresh water. As countries become more developed and people’s living standards rise, they use more water in their daily lives, for example for washing machines, dishwashers, and swimming pools.

There are several ways to address this problem. Governments should invest in better water infrastructure, such as fixing old pipes that leak water. They should also encourage farmers to use more efficient irrigation methods, like drip irrigation, which uses much less water. Laws should also be made to punish companies that pollute rivers and lakes. As individuals, we can all make small but important changes, such as taking shorter showers, fixing dripping taps quickly, and saving rainwater for gardening.

In conclusion, the growing demand for fresh water is caused by population growth, intensive farming, and industrialisation. With cooperation between governments and individuals, we can manage our water resources more carefully and ensure there is enough for future generations.
